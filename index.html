Sh. Bhagat Singh

1907-1931

Sahid-e-Azam

Bhagat Singh (27 September 1907[1] – 23 March 1931) was a charismatic Indian revolutionary[3] who participated in the mistaken murder of a junior British police officer[4] in what was to be retaliation for the death of an Indian nationalist.[5] He later took part in a largely symbolic bombing of the Central Legislative Assembly in Delhi and a hunger strike in jail, which—on the back of sympathetic coverage in Indian-owned newspapers—turned him into a household name in the Punjab region, and after his execution at age 23 into a martyr and folk hero in Northern India.[6] Borrowing ideas from Bolshevism and anarchism,[7] he electrified a growing militancy in India in the 1930s, and prompted urgent introspection within the Indian National Congress's nonviolent but eventually successful campaign for India's independence.

Important Points

  • Early life: Bhagat Singh was born on September 28, 1907, in a small village in Punjab, which is now in Pakistan. He came from a family deeply involved in the freedom movement, and his father and uncles were also active participants.
  • Influence of Jallianwala Bagh Massacre: The Jallianwala Bagh Massacre in 1919 deeply affected Bhagat Singh at a young age. The incident, where British soldiers killed unarmed Indian protesters, left a lasting impression on his mind and ignited his nationalist fervor.
  • Joining the freedom movement: Bhagat Singh joined the freedom movement at a young age and was associated with several revolutionary organizations, including the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA).
  • Role in the Kakori Conspiracy: Bhagat Singh, along with other revolutionaries, played a significant role in the Kakori train robbery in 1925, where they looted a train carrying government funds to finance their revolutionary activities.
  • Non-cooperation movement: During the Non-Cooperation Movement launched by Mahatma Gandhi in 1920, Bhagat Singh left his school and joined the movement, giving up his formal education in the process.
  • Lala Lajpat Rai's death: In 1928, during the protests against the Simon Commission, Lala Lajpat Rai was brutally beaten by the police. Though he survived the injuries initially, he later died, which led Bhagat Singh and his associates to seek revenge against the police officer responsible.

  • The trial and execution: Bhagat Singh, along with Rajguru and Sukhdev, was tried for the murder of John Saunders and sentenced to death. Despite widespread public protests and appeals for clemency, they were executed on March 23, 1931, in Lahore Central Jail. The day is now commemorated as Shaheed Diwas (Martyrs' Day) in India.Bhagat Singh's writings: During his time in jail, Bhagat Singh wrote extensively on various social and political issues.
